Methodology

We randomly chose 10 Hall of Famers as well as 10 great players who did not make it to the Hall of Fame and compared their career stats. We mainly used R to analyze our data, because clustering is easier in that language and we didn’t have enough experience in another language with the ability to analyze data. Also, we did all of the data cleaning in R because it was faster than methods including python. Then, we color-coded the players (Hall of Fame as blue, others as red) and made scatter plots comparing important statistics.




Graphs

A player’s VORP and points per game show how much better they are than their designated replacement and how much (on avg) they scored a game. Most Hall of Famers have a higher VORP and PPG than the other players, but not all.

A comparison between all players’ VORP and win shares showed that most Hall of Famers have higher values than the other players.

A comparison between all players’ win shares and efficiency rating showed that the Hall of Famers have higher values than the other players.

A player’s efficiency rating and their points per game is indicative of their offensive ability. The Hall of Famers have a higher PER and PPG, with some exceptions.




Other Factors

Though stats are an important indicator of a player’s skill, that is not the sole reason players get into the Hall of Fame. Sometimes players with the best stats never get to win a championship, or a player’s stats do not represent their skill because of their play style, like Steve Nash. He was a great playmaker who helped all of his teammates play better. Before Nash joined the Suns, they went 29-53, but the year after he joined they went 62-20.
